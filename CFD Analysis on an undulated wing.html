CFD Analysis on an undulated wing

A Comparative CFD Study on Laminar & Turbulent Flow, Turbulence Models, and Angles of Attack.

Project Summary:

Objective: To conduct a high-fidelity Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) study investigating the aerodynamic performance of a biomimetic airfoil. The project aimed to validate experimental findings that showed increased lift for a NACA 0012 airfoil with leading-edge undulations (inspired by humpback whale flippers) compared to theoretical predictions, by analyzing flow behavior across multiple angles of attack and turbulence models.

Core Challenge: Resolve the discrepancy between classical aerodynamic theory—which predicts identical lift for the same airfoil profile—and initial experimental data showing enhanced lift from the undulated design. Quantify the impact on lift (CL), drag (CD), and complex flow phenomena.

Outcome: Successfully executed a complete, industry-standard CFD validation workflow. Performed a rigorous grid convergence study, compared turbulence models, and visualized complex flow fields, providing verified force data and insights into the vortex-generating mechanism of the undulations.

Methodology & Technical Execution:

1. Geometric Modeling:
• Base Design: A NACA 0012 airfoil was modified with a selected sinusoidal leading-edge undulation pattern.
• Domain Creation: Utilized "cavitation" technique within CAD/CFD pre-processing. A solid 0.2m x 0.2m x 0.3m rectangular domain was created, and the undulated wing geometry was subtracted from it. This resulted in a perfectly conforming fluid volume (the "negative" of the wing), ensuring precise and watertight boundary condition application on the wing surface.
• Simulation Matrix: Configured cases for 0°, 4°, and 6° angles of attack to systematically evaluate performance across operational ranges.


2. CFD Simulation Setup:

Solver & Software:Utilized the CRADLE CFD software suite for 3D, steady-state, turbulent flow simulations.
Flow Conditions:Simulated air at standard atmospheric conditions. A key parameter was setting the Reynolds Number to 200,000 to match the low-Re experimental context, with a specified low inlet turbulence intensity of 0.1%.
Turbulence Modeling Study:To ensure robust conclusions and assess model sensitivity, simulations were duplicated using two distinct Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) models:
   • Standard k-ε Model: For general turbulent flow prediction.
   • SST k-ω Model: For improved accuracy in capturing flow separation and adverse pressure gradients, particularly relevant for the undulated leading edge.

3. Meshing Strategy:

An octree meshing technique was employed, with significant effort dedicated to creating progressively finer meshes (ranging from ~234k to over 5.5 million nodes) for the convergence study. Boundary layers were refined to accurately capture viscous effects.

4. Analysis Parameters:

Simulations were run at multiple angles of attack (0°, 4°, 6°). Convergence was meticulously monitored by tracking the stabilization of pressure and velocity variables over hundreds of iterations.

5. Post-Processing & Validation: Key results extracted included:
   • Pressure coefficient (Cᴾ) contours to identify high/low-pressure zones.
   • Velocity vector fields and streamlines to visualize flow patterns, separation, and vortex generation.
   • Calculation of lift and drag forces (Fᴸ, Fᴰ) and their non-dimensional coefficients (Cᴸ, Cᴰ).
   • Implementation of a Grid Convergence Study to quantify numerical uncertainty and establish mesh independence.

Key Findings & Results:

Convergence Validation:

All simulations achieved stable convergence, typically within 200-540 cycles, confirming the reliability of the results.

Grid Independence:

The grid convergence study confirmed result reliability .At 0° Angle of Attack, the undulated wing exhibited a very low drag coefficient (Cᴰ ~ 0.00024 to 0.0015 depending on mesh), confirming a streamlined design.

Flow Physics:
• Pressure Distribution: High pressure was consistently observed at the leading-edge stagnation point, with the undulations creating a more complex pressure footprint compared to the smooth wing.

• Vortex Management: Streamline and vector plots revealed that the tubercles channeled flow and generated controlled vortices, which appeared to delay stall-like effects and smooth the pressure distribution along the span.

• Changing the Angle of Attack directly impacted performance. For instance, at 4° AOA with the k-ε model, Cᴸ increased to 0.00085, demonstrating lift generation.


• Turbulence Model Comparison: The SST k-ω model, as expected, predicted different force distributions than the k-ε model, particularly at higher angles of attack (e.g., Cᴸ=0.00211 at 6° AOA), highlighting the importance of model selection for accurate separation prediction.
• Comprehensive Performance Data: A full dataset of lift and drag coefficients was generated for both smooth and undulated profiles across the tested angles of attack, providing a quantitative basis for comparison with theoretical and experimental data.
